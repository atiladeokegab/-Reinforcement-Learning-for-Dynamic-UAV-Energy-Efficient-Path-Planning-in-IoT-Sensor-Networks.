\subsection{Existing Optimisation Approaches}

Zorbas et al.~\cite{zorbas2025revisiting} propose a Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) framework for optimal SF allocation, formulated as an integer programme over the SF assignment vector with total collision probability as the objective. For a fixed network snapshot it gives a globally optimal assignment. Two properties limit it in a mobile UAV setting. First, it scales exponentially with sensor count and becomes intractable at $N{\ge}30$ without decomposition. Second, it treats each link's RSSI as a fixed parameter. That assumption breaks when a mobile UAV and the EMA-ADR loop are continuously shifting effective link quality at each sensor: the optimal SF assignment at step $t$ is not the same at $t+1$, and a static programme cannot account for the trajectory's temporal structure.

Xiong et al.~\cite{xiong2023flyinglora} introduce FlyingLoRa, which
minimises energy by jointly optimising the UAV's 3D trajectory and
transmission scheduling offline, given full knowledge of sensor
locations and link budgets before the mission. FlyingLoRa omits ADR
dynamics (treating each sensor's rate as fixed) and does not model
the Capture Effect, and therefore cannot exploit the
positioning-induced SF diversity that the protocol-aware DRL agent
learns to generate. The channel-geometry and energy-consumption
assumptions that underpin this work follow the UAV-communications
tutorials by Zeng et al.~\cite{zeng2016wireless},
Mozaffari et al.~\cite{mozaffari2019tutorial}, and
Zeng and Zhang~\cite{zeng2017energy}.

Challita et al.~\cite{challita2018interference} apply deep RL to
interference-aware path planning for cellular-connected UAVs: their
learned policy cuts wireless latency by 47\% and raises ground-user
throughput by 37\% against a shortest-path baseline. Nemer et
al.~\cite{nemer2022energyefficient} use a distributed actor--critic
for multi-UAV coverage fairness, with Jain's Fairness Index as the
target metric. Neither study models the non-stationary MAC-layer
dynamics (EMA-ADR, duty cycles, Capture Effect) that make LoRaWAN
structurally harder. Liu et al.~\cite{liu2019uav} are representative:
DDPG over a flat concatenated state vector of per-PoI coverage scores
and energy values, $N$ fixed at training time. A flat MLP has a fixed
input width, so any sensor-count change requires a new training run.
The DQN versus policy-gradient argument for this reward landscape is
in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_methodology}.
