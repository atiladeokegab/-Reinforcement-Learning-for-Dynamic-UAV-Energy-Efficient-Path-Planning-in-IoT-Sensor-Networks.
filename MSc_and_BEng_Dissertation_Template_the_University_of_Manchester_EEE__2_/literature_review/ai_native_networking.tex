\subsection{DRL and UAV Path Planning}

Nguyen et al.~\cite{nguyen20226g} outline the 6G vision of AI-native
networks where DRL manages massive heterogeneous IoT connectivity in
real time, conditions under which hand-crafted optimisation policies
cannot scale. The Deep Q-Network~\cite{mnih2015humanlevel} stabilises
Q-learning with experience replay and a periodically frozen target
network; replay breaks the temporal correlation of consecutive UAV
observations and the target network prevents the Q-value feedback
loop that causes divergence. Luong et al.~\cite{luong2019drl} survey
DRL in communications and observe that reward shaping and state
design are critical, directly motivating the frame-stacking and
urgency-proxy design choices in Section~\ref{sec:method}.

No prior work applies DRL specifically to UAV path planning in
LoRaWAN networks with explicit ADR and Capture Effect modelling. The
closest precedents, Challita et al.~\cite{challita2018interference}
and Nemer et al.~\cite{nemer2022energyefficient}, operate in the
cellular domain where the interference field is determined solely by
geometry and can be estimated from a single observation. In LoRaWAN
the SF assignment of each sensor depends on its RSSI history
filtered through the EMA-ADR loop: the same geometric configuration
can yield entirely different SF distributions depending on the UAV's
prior trajectory. This history-dependence violates the Markov
property and forces the agent to infer latent protocol state from
temporal sequences rather than instantaneous observations, a
qualitatively harder learning problem than the stationary
interference settings in prior work. The architectural-selection
argument for MLP over attention or graph neural networks on this
specific training distribution is summarised in
Appendix~\ref{app:extended_methodology}.

On information freshness and domain generalisation, Kaul et al.~\cite{kaul2012realtime} show that the throughput-optimal
update frequency is not the freshness-optimal frequency, motivating
the explicit AoI term in the reward. Abd-Elmagid et
al.~\cite{abdelmagid2019aoi} demonstrate a fundamental AoI--throughput
trade-off in IoT networks, supporting the multi-term weighted reward.
Tobin et al.~\cite{tobin2017domain} introduce domain randomisation
to bridge the sim-to-real gap (subsequently validated at scale in
robotic manipulation~\cite{openai2019dexterous}), and Bengio et
al.~\cite{bengio2009curriculum} show curriculum learning accelerates
convergence and improves generalisation; both techniques are combined
in this work to produce a single policy deployable across a
$100\times$ variation in operational area without retraining.
