\subsection{The Shift to AI-Native Networking}

To overcome the limitations of static and heuristic methods, the field is moving toward
intelligent, autonomous network management. Nguyen et al.~\cite{nguyen20226g} outline the
vision for 6G IoT, predicting a shift toward ``AI-native'' networks where Deep Reinforcement
Learning (DRL) and edge intelligence play a central role in managing massive connectivity
and ultra-reliable low-latency communications. A central argument of their survey is that
the scale and dynamism of next-generation IoT---billions of heterogeneous devices with
time-varying channel conditions---makes hand-crafted optimisation policies fundamentally
insufficient; only agents that can learn from interaction with the environment can keep
pace. This paradigm motivates the DRL approach taken in this dissertation.

\subsubsection{Deep Q-Networks and Their Application to Communications}

The foundational algorithm underpinning this shift is the Deep Q-Network
(DQN)~\cite{mnih2015humanlevel}. Mnih et al.\ demonstrated that by combining Q-learning
with a deep convolutional neural network, experience replay, and a periodically frozen
target network, an agent could learn superhuman policies for 49 Atari games directly from
raw pixel input---a result that established DRL as a practical tool for high-dimensional
control. The two key stabilisation techniques introduced in that work are directly relevant
to the networking setting: \emph{experience replay} breaks the temporal correlation between
consecutive training samples (important in a UAV mission where consecutive observations are
spatially and temporally correlated), and the \emph{target network} prevents the feedback
loop between the predicted and target Q-values that otherwise causes divergence during
training.

Luong et al.~\cite{luong2019drl} provide a comprehensive survey of DRL applications in
communications and networking across five domains: dynamic network access, data rate
control, wireless caching, data offloading, and network security. Of particular relevance
to this dissertation are their findings on data collection in IoT networks, where they
observe that DRL agents consistently outperform static schedulers and greedy heuristics
under variable channel conditions, but that reward shaping and state space design are
critical: poorly chosen state representations prevent the agent from learning the temporal
patterns needed to anticipate future congestion. This motivates the frame-stacking and
urgency-proxy design choices in this work, discussed in \autoref{sec:method}.

\subsubsection{DRL for UAV Path Planning}

The application of DRL specifically to UAV trajectory planning in wireless networks has
gained significant traction. Challita et al.~\cite{challita2018interference} formulate the
joint path planning and interference management problem for multiple cellular-connected UAVs
as a noncooperative game, solved via a deep RL algorithm based on echo state network (ESN)
cells. Their key finding is that a DRL policy can learn to trade off wireless latency
against interference caused to ground users, achieving a Nash equilibrium that reduces
per-UAV latency by up to 33\% compared to shortest-path navigation. This result is directly
analogous to the trade-off addressed in this dissertation: the DRL agent must trade off
spatial coverage (which requires movement) against protocol-layer quality (which requires
hovering to stabilise LoRaWAN ADR). Challita et al.\ also demonstrate that the ESN-based
agent generalises to unseen network states, supporting the case for DRL over look-up-table
approaches.

Nemer et al.~\cite{nemer2022energyefficient} extend this line of work to the fairness
dimension, proposing a distributed actor--critic algorithm (SBG-AC) for multi-UAV coverage
that simultaneously optimises coverage score, energy consumption, and Jain's Fairness Index
over ground users. Their results show that fairness-constrained DRL outperforms
energy-only DRL, confirming that the reward function must explicitly penalise unequal
service to prevent the agent converging on a policy that maximises aggregate throughput at
the expense of underserved nodes. This finding directly motivates the Age-of-Information
penalty term in the reward function of this work: without an explicit fairness cost, a DRL
agent trained only on throughput would learn to camp over high-rate (SF7) sensors and
neglect distant SF12 nodes.

\subsubsection{Age of Information as a Freshness Objective}

A complementary strand of literature has formalised the concept of \emph{information
freshness} through the Age of Information (AoI) metric. Kaul et al.~\cite{kaul2012realtime}
introduced AoI as the time elapsed since the last successfully received status update,
showing that the throughput-optimal update frequency is not the freshness-optimal
frequency---a system can maximise throughput while delivering stale information if bursts
of updates are lost or delayed. This decoupling of throughput and freshness is directly
relevant to UAV-LoRaWAN data collection: an agent that maximises bytes collected can
achieve high aggregate throughput while starving distant sensors of any service,
accumulating unbounded AoI at those nodes.

Abd-Elmagid et al.~\cite{abdelmagid2019aoi} extend this analysis to energy-harvesting IoT
networks, demonstrating a fundamental trade-off between the AoI-optimal scheduling policy
and the throughput-optimal policy. Their result implies that a single scalar reward
combining throughput and AoI will generally learn a compromise policy that is suboptimal on
both individual objectives---supporting the multi-term, weighted reward function design
adopted in this work. Tripathi and Modiano~\cite{tripathi2022aoi} further show that when
sources are spatially or statistically correlated (as they are in a sensor field where
geographically proximate sensors tend to be assigned similar SFs under ADR), the
AoI-optimal policy must account for this correlation structure, an insight that informs the
use of per-sensor urgency vectors (rather than a single aggregate AoI) in the state space.

\subsubsection{Generalisation via Curriculum Learning and Domain Randomisation}

A persistent challenge in applying DRL to real-world wireless systems is the
\emph{generalisation} problem: policies trained on a fixed network topology or channel
distribution often fail catastrophically when deployed on even slightly different
conditions. Bengio et al.~\cite{bengio2009curriculum} demonstrate that organising training
examples by difficulty---beginning with simple cases and progressively introducing harder
ones---produces policies that learn faster and generalise better than policies trained on
the full difficulty distribution from the outset. Their theoretical argument is that easy
examples provide a strong learning signal early in training, building a foundation that
enables the agent to later distinguish meaningful structure in hard examples.

Tobin et al.~\cite{tobin2017domain} introduce Domain Randomisation as a complementary
technique for bridging the Sim-to-Real gap: by randomising the physical parameters of the
simulation environment during training, the policy is forced to learn invariances that hold
across all realisations, rather than overfitting to a single simulator configuration.
OpenAI~\cite{openai2019dexterous} subsequently applied Domain Randomisation at scale to
learn dexterous robot hand manipulation from simulation, transferring policies trained
entirely in simulation to a physical robot---demonstrating that the technique is effective
even under very large sim-to-real gaps. In the UAV-LoRaWAN setting, the analogous gap
arises from unmodelled propagation effects (multipath, shadowing variance, GPS error); by
randomising sensor positions, grid sizes, and channel parameters during training, this
dissertation's Domain Randomisation regime produces a single policy deployable across a
100$\times$ variation in operational area without retraining.
