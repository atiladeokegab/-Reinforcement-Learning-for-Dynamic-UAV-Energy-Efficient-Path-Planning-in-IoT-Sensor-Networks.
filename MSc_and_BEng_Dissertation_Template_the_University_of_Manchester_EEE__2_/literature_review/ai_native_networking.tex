\subsection{The Shift to AI-Native Networking}

Nguyen et al.~\cite{nguyen20226g} outline the 6G vision of AI-native networks where DRL
manages massive, heterogeneous IoT connectivity in real time, conditions under which
hand-crafted optimisation policies cannot scale.

\subsubsection{Deep Q-Networks in Communications}

The Deep Q-Network~\cite{mnih2015humanlevel} stabilises Q-learning with experience replay
and a periodically frozen target network. Experience replay breaks the temporal correlation
between consecutive UAV observations (important given the spatial correlation of the flight
path), while the target network prevents the Q-value feedback loop that causes divergence.
Luong et al.~\cite{luong2019drl} survey DRL in communications and observe that reward
shaping and state-space design are critical: poorly chosen representations prevent the agent
from learning the temporal patterns needed to anticipate future congestion. This directly
motivates the frame-stacking and urgency-proxy design choices in~\autoref{sec:method}.

The non-stationary reward landscape introduced by EMA-ADR convergence presents a
particular stability challenge that on-policy methods struggle to handle. Because the
same state--action pair yields different rewards depending on the accumulated ADR history,
on-policy algorithms such as PPO discard relevant transitions before the ADR feedback loop
has resolved. DQN's experience replay retains these transitions across ADR timescales,
allowing the agent to correlate dwell behaviour with the delayed SF-downgrade reward
that follows, a temporal gap of approximately $1/\lambda = 10$ steps that one-step
lookahead cannot bridge. This property is central to why off-policy value-based learning
is preferred over policy-gradient alternatives in the LoRaWAN setting.

\subsubsection{DRL for UAV Path Planning}

No prior work applies DRL specifically to UAV path planning in LoRaWAN networks with
explicit ADR and Capture Effect modelling. The closest precedents (Challita et
al.~\cite{challita2018interference}, Nemer et al.~\cite{nemer2022energyefficient}) operate
in the cellular domain where interference models are stationary and the non-stationary
EMA-ADR feedback loop is absent. This gap is the primary motivation for this dissertation.

A further structural difference from prior DRL path-planning work is the coupling between
physical positioning and MAC-layer protocol state. In cellular interference avoidance, the
interference field is determined solely by geometry and can be estimated from a single
observation. In LoRaWAN, the SF assignment of each sensor depends on its RSSI history
filtered through the EMA-ADR loop: the same geometric configuration can yield entirely
different SF distributions depending on the UAV's prior trajectory. This history-dependence
violates the Markov property and means the agent must infer latent protocol state from
temporal sequences rather than instantaneous observations, a qualitatively harder
learning problem than the stationary interference settings considered by Challita et al.\
and Nemer et al.

\subsubsection{Architecture Selection: MLP versus Attention and Graph Neural Networks}
\label{sec:arch_selection}

The choice of a fixed-capacity Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) as the policy backbone warrants
explicit justification, since attention-based and graph neural network (GNN) architectures
have demonstrated advantages in combinatorial routing tasks that share structural
similarities with UAV coverage planning.

Kool et al.~\cite{kool2018attention} show that attention mechanisms can learn
permutation-invariant policies over variable-length sets of locations, a natural fit for
problems where the agent must sequence visits across an unordered sensor deployment.
Cappart et al.~\cite{cappart2023combinatorial} demonstrate that GNN policies that
explicitly represent the deployment graph can generalise across topology changes that
defeat fixed-input architectures. In principle, either approach could encode inter-sensor
spatial relationships that the MLP with concatenated per-sensor features cannot.

However, several properties of the primary evaluation regime favour the MLP. First, the
UAV's five-action discrete space is low-dimensional; the representational bottleneck lies
in the temporal protocol state, which frame stacking addresses without architectural
complexity. Second, the training distribution (uniformly random sensor positions sampled
each episode) does not impose consistent inter-sensor topology, so a graph-structured
inductive bias offers no stable structure to exploit. The cross-layout generalisation
analysis (\autoref{sec:robustness}) confirms this: the MLP achieves statistically
significant advantages on geometrically regular layouts (grid-aligned, ring) and matches
or exceeds greedy performance on dispersed layouts analogous to the training distribution.
Third, MLP compatibility with the Stable-Baselines3 DQN implementation avoids the
engineering overhead of custom graph-batch collation in the replay buffer, which would
complicate the training pipeline without clear benefit for the primary regime.

The failure boundary is precisely identified: bimodal two-cluster deployments ($-14.0\%$,
$d = 5.33$, \autoref{sec:robustness}) represent a topological regime where
inter-cluster routing decisions cannot be inferred from local positional features. A
curriculum fine-tuning experiment (\autoref{sec:clustered_curriculum}) confirms the gap is
architectural rather than distributional: the MLP cannot encode cluster identity or
inter-cluster routing priority regardless of training exposure. This establishes attention
or GNN architectures as a motivated next step rather than a speculative one, and the
performance gap quantifies the representational cost of the MLP choice in this specific
failure regime.

\subsubsection{Information Freshness and Domain Generalisation}

Kaul et al.~\cite{kaul2012realtime} show that the throughput-optimal update frequency is not
the freshness-optimal frequency, motivating the explicit AoI term in the reward function.
Abd-Elmagid et al.~\cite{abdelmagid2019aoi} demonstrate a fundamental AoI--throughput
trade-off in IoT networks, supporting the multi-term weighted reward design adopted here.

Tobin et al.~\cite{tobin2017domain} introduce Domain Randomisation to bridge the sim-to-real
gap, subsequently validated by OpenAI~\cite{openai2019dexterous} at scale in robotic
manipulation. Bengio et al.~\cite{bengio2009curriculum} show that curriculum learning, which presents
training examples in order of increasing difficulty, produces faster convergence and better
generalisation. Both techniques are combined in this work to produce a single policy
deployable across a 100$\times$ variation in operational area without retraining.
