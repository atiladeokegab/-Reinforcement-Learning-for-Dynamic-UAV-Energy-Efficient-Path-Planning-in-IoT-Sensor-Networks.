\subsection{LoRaWAN Scalability and the SF Monoculture}

LoRaWAN is the de facto standard for long-range, low-power IoT
connectivity. Augustin et al.~\cite{augustin2016lora} characterise
the chirp spread-spectrum modulation, the six orthogonal spreading
factors $\mathrm{SF}\in\{7,\ldots,12\}$, and the rate--budget
trade-off that governs SF selection. Bor et al.~\cite{bor2016lora}
show through large-scale simulation that a single gateway can
theoretically serve thousands of devices but that this capacity is
highly sensitive to the SF distribution: if too many devices converge
on the same high-SF setting, per-device throughput collapses due to
intra-SF collisions and the resulting long Time-on-Air.

A critical failure mode in mobile LoRaWAN networks is the congestion
collapse known as the SF monoculture or ``SF12 Well.'' Zorbas et
al.~\cite{zorbas2025revisiting} analyse this failure rigorously:
because the standard ADR mechanism reacts to smoothed RSSI history,
devices in a rapidly changing environment (a UAV moving away)
accumulate outdated estimates and converge on high SFs long after
the UAV has left, driving the entire network toward SF12. The result
is self-reinforcing: high SF increases collision probability,
reducing successful transmissions, which in turn prevents downgrade
commands from being received. Reynders et
al.~\cite{reynders2016range} provide complementary evidence that
collision probability grows super-linearly beyond a critical load
threshold, motivating the ADR modelling choices in this work.

The Capture Effect offers a partial remedy to this. Croce et al.~\cite{croce2020lora} are the first to rigorously
characterise the LoRa Capture Effect through software-defined radio
experiments. They show that collisions between two LoRa signals on
the same SF can be resolved if the stronger signal exceeds the
weaker by a power ratio of approximately $6$\,dB, the ``capture
threshold''; below this threshold both packets are lost. This
overturns the common simplifying assumption in prior LoRaWAN
simulators (that any intra-SF collision destroys all packets) and
creates a tension between coverage (demands high SFs at range) and
capacity (demands low SFs for throughput) that a mobile UAV can
resolve dynamically: closing the physical distance to sensors
reduces path loss, enables SF downgrade via ADR, and creates the
power differential required for capture.

This project directly incorporates the $6$\,dB threshold of
Croce et al.~\cite{croce2020lora} into the collision-resolution
logic, rewarding the agent for reaching spatial positions that
induce the power differential required for capture, a design
distinction from all protocol-blind trajectory planners.
