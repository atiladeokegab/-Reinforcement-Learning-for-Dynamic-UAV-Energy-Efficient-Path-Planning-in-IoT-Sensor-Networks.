\section{Project Management}
\label{sec:project_management}

\subsection*{Planning and Execution}

The project was planned at the outset using a five-milestone Gantt chart (Appendix~\ref{app:gantt}), covering environment development, initial DQN training, the Domain Randomisation and Curriculum Learning regime, multi-seed evaluation, and dissertation write-up. The plan was structured across two semesters with natural checkpoints aligned to the Progress Review Meetings.

The project broadly followed the original timeline, with two notable deviations. First, the scope of the evaluation was expanded during Semester 2: the original plan specified a single-condition baseline comparison, but preliminary results revealed high inter-seed variance in the DQN agent at $N \geq 20$. A five-seed, multi-condition evaluation was therefore adopted to provide statistically defensible conclusions, shifting the evaluation phase by approximately two weeks. Second, the introduction of the SF-Aware Max-Throughput Greedy baseline was not planned initially; it was added during Semester 2 after recognising that the Nearest-Sensor Greedy alone could not isolate the contribution of long-horizon planning from the contribution of protocol awareness. Both deviations increased the quality of the final results and were discussed with the Project Supervisor.

\subsection*{Risk Management}

Risks were tracked throughout the project using the Risk Register in Appendix~\ref{app:risk}. Two risks materialised during execution. Training divergence occurred during early DQN experiments on the full curriculum (large grids, $N = 40$), manifesting as reward collapse after approximately $5 \times 10^5$ steps. This was resolved by introducing gradient clipping (max norm 10), reducing the learning rate from $10^{-3}$ to $5 \times 10^{-4}$, and applying reward scaling. The second materialised risk was time overrun in the evaluation phase, mitigated by restricting the grid-size sweep to $N = 20$ sensors and five representative grid sizes rather than the full combinatorial grid originally considered.

\subsection*{Self-Directed Learning and CPD}

The project required substantial self-directed learning across two technical domains not covered in the taught curriculum: LoRaWAN protocol internals and deep reinforcement learning implementation. A CPD Log recording these activities is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:cpd}. Key learning activities included a structured study of the LoRaWAN 1.0.4 specification to correctly implement the ADR mechanism, study of the Stable-Baselines3 DQN source code to understand target network update schedules, and independent reading of the primary literature on the Capture Effect and domain randomisation. The modular \texttt{.tex} file structure adopted for the dissertation allowed writing to proceed in parallel with experimental work, reducing the risk of a late-stage write-up bottleneck.
