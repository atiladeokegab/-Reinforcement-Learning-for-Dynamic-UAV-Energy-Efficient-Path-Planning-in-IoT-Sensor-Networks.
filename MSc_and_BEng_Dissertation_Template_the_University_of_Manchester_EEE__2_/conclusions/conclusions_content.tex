\subsection{Conclusions}

This project builds and evaluates a DRL framework for UAV-assisted LoRaWAN data collection,
coupling UAV trajectory planning with LoRaWAN protocol physics in a Gymnasium environment.
Systematic evaluation across a $100{\times}$ area range ($100{\times}100$ to
$1{,}000{\times}1{,}000$ cells) reveals that the operative constraint on single-UAV
performance is not algorithm quality but physical feasibility: energy and link-budget limits
define a hard boundary that no policy can overcome. Six contributions result.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Simulation environment}: the first open Gymnasium framework modelling the
    complete causal chain from UAV position through RSSI, EMA-ADR, and Capture Effect to
    packet delivery, with Two-Ray path loss, log-normal shadowing, asynchronous duty cycles,
    and a physically motivated link-budget constraint ($r_{\max} \approx 365$ grid units at
    SF12, Table~\ref{tab:sf_range}).

    \item \textbf{Curriculum training}: a three-stage domain-randomisation curriculum
    producing a single policy that operates across all 16 tested conditions (4 grid sizes
    $\times$ 4 sensor counts) without retraining, spanning trivially solvable
    ($100{\times}100$) through physically infeasible ($1{,}000{\times}1{,}000$) regimes.

    \item \textbf{Multi-objective reward}: AoI urgency, buffer-overflow penalties, a
    discovery bonus, and an energy term prevent degenerate hovering and drive the agent
    to navigate toward out-of-range sensors. Ablation confirms AoI shapes training
    behaviour without being observable at inference ($d = 0.005$, $p = 0.987$).

    \item \textbf{SF-Aware Greedy baseline}: a novel protocol-aware comparator that
    routes deterministically toward the lowest-SF sensor. This baseline proves harder to
    exceed than standard routing heuristics, outperforming the DQN at 300$\times$300
    ($d = 1.65$, $p = 0.052$) and 500$\times$500 ($d = 1.84$, $p = 0.039$), and
    quantifying the performance ceiling achievable by explicit SF-aware routing.

    \item \textbf{Operational envelope characterisation}: systematic evaluation identifies
    three regimes, trivial ($100{\times}100$, all agents J$>$0.96), feasible
    ($300{\times}300$, SFGreedy leads with J$\approx$0.55), and infeasible
    ($1{,}000{\times}1{,}000$, all agents NDR$<$13\%), and the single-UAV physical
    boundary at which energy and range make full coverage impossible regardless of policy.

    \item \textbf{Emergent dwell-then-reposition}: despite not matching SFGreedy on
    aggregate metrics, the DQN learns to sustain proximity for $\sim$10 steps near sensor
    clusters to drive EMA-ADR convergence before repositioning. This temporal strategy is
    structurally inaccessible to one-step greedy look-ahead and represents genuine
    long-horizon protocol-aware learning.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Key Findings.}

The central finding is that single-UAV LoRaWAN data collection faces a physical feasibility
ceiling, not an algorithmic one. At $1{,}000{\times}1{,}000$, no agent achieves more than
12\% per-sensor coverage: the grid diagonal ($14{,}142$\,m) exceeds the maximum traversable
distance within the episode energy budget ($d_{\max} \approx 15{,}782$\,m with 20\% reserve),
and the TSP tour to visit all 20 sensors ($\approx 44{,}700$\,m) is a factor of 2.8 beyond
what the UAV can achieve. This boundary is independent of policy quality.

Within the feasible regime ($300{\times}300$ and below), SF-Aware Greedy demonstrates that
explicit protocol-awareness in routing, routing toward the lowest-SF sensor, is more
effective than the DQN's learned policy at the current training budget. The DQN performs
comparably to Nearest-Sensor Greedy, confirming that the key capability gap is the
absence of structured sensor-topology representation in the MLP policy. The dwell-then-reposition
behaviour shows the agent has learned \emph{when} to exploit ADR convergence, but not yet
\emph{where} to route to maximise its cumulative effect. Closing this gap is the specific
motivation for the attention-based and graph-neural-network extensions identified as
highest priority in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.
