\subsection{Conclusions}

This dissertation coupled UAV trajectory planning with the full LoRaWAN physics stack inside a Gymnasium simulation, trained a DQN agent under domain randomisation and a competence-gated curriculum, and used the resulting framework to diagnose and then resolve an architectural limit of flat-policy learners on this problem.

Five contributions came out of this work. The environment (Section~\ref{sec:env}) is the first open simulator to couple Two-Ray path loss, log-normal shadowing, EMA-based ADR, and the LoRa Capture Effect into a single per-timestep causal chain; prior work omits at least one of the four. The curriculum (Section~\ref{sec:training}) produces a single DQN policy covering all $(\text{grid}, N)$ combinations without retraining; the four-condition ablation confirms AoI features are load-bearing and reveals that easy curriculum stages suppress large-scale performance through negative transfer. A 50-seed five-condition comparison (Section~\ref{sec:multi_seed}) against Nearest-Sensor Greedy, SF-Aware Greedy, and a TSP Oracle establishes the operational envelope and routing-efficiency ceiling. The architectural diagnostic (Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic}) shows that median-zero wall collisions alongside $100.0\%$ boundary-cell occupancy is the signature of a representational ceiling, not a training failure. Replacing the flat-MLP trunk with a permutation-invariant Deep-Sets encoder (Section~\ref{sec:relational}) removes the boundary-hugging pathology and outperforms the DQN at every condition beyond $N{=}20$ (Table~\ref{tab:scalability_ndr}).

The scalability sweep points to three regimes: trivial (any agent succeeds), policy-sensitive (routing quality determines the outcome), and battery-bounded (no agent can visit every sensor within the episode budget). The feasibility boundary is set by physics, not policy quality. The architectural diagnostic pins the DQN failure to missing permutation invariance, and usefully the same diagnosis predicts the fix. Switching to a permutation-invariant encoder removes the boundary-hugging pathology; retraining the DQN with the same reward supplements shifted NDR by at most $1\%$ across all five conditions, which confirms the gap is architectural. The diagnostic-then-fix pairing is the main contribution.

The evaluation has two limitations. The boundary diagnostic ran at only one $(200{\times}200, N{=}20)$ condition across 20 seeds; extending it across the full five-condition sweep would establish whether edge-bias is uniform or a threshold phenomenon tied to sensor density. The Lawnmower baseline uses only 5 seeds and serves as a structural reference, not a statistically grounded comparison.

The head-to-head comparison between DQN and Relational-RL changes two things at once: the inductive bias of the architecture (flat MLP versus set encoder) and the training algorithm (DQN versus PPO). Both affect performance, and the current results cannot separate their individual contributions. Resolving it would require PPO with the flat MLP and DQN with the set encoder on the same curriculum; that experiment was not run and is listed in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

The simulation is 2D at fixed $100$\,m altitude with no 3D channel effects modelled. Real LoRa deployments encounter building reflections, obstructions, and ground-level path-loss exponents that differ substantially from the open-field Two-Ray model used here. The EMA-ADR implementation uses four Spreading Factors rather than the six in the LoRaWAN standard; the trade-off is protocol fidelity against a tractable state space and cleaner SF-diversity analysis. Both choices limit how directly the quantitative NDR figures translate to a hardware deployment, though the architectural finding that permutation invariance is necessary for scaling does not depend on these simulation parameters.
