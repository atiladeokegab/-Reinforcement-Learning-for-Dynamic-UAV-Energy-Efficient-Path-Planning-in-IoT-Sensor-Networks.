Mobile UAV gateways in dense LoRaWAN sensor fields face two linked problems. Standard flat-policy reinforcement learners lack the inductive bias needed to treat the sensor field as an unordered set, and rapid link-quality changes drive Adaptive Data Rate (ADR) convergence onto a single Spreading Factor, collapsing packet delivery. This dissertation addresses both by coupling UAV trajectory planning with the full LoRaWAN physics stack inside a Gymnasium simulation, training a Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent with domain randomisation and a competence-gated curriculum, then using direct empirical analysis to identify the architectural ceiling of a flat-MLP policy.

The simulation models the complete causal chain from UAV position through Two-Ray path loss, log-normal shadowing, EMA-based ADR, and the LoRa Capture Effect to per-packet delivery within a single environment step. A single policy trained across five (grid size, sensor count) conditions -- from $100{\times}100$ to $500{\times}500$ grids with $N \in \{10, 20, 30, 40, 50\}$ sensors -- is evaluated against Nearest Sensor Greedy, SF-Aware Greedy, and a TSP Oracle using 50 independent seeds per condition. A four-condition ablation across five scales (50 seeds each) shows that AoI observation features are load-bearing at every tested density (NDR falls from $13.0\%$ to $4.4\%$ at $500{\times}500$/$N{=}50$, $d{=}1.07$) and exposes a negative-transfer effect: easy training conditions depress large-scale performance, with a single-environment specialist outperforming the domain-randomised generalist ($d{=}-1.69$, $p{<}10^{-12}$).

The central finding is architectural: the trained DQN produces two consistent behaviours, sustained in-place dwell at sensor clusters and boundary-adjacent flight, which quantitative diagnostics (mean 21.8 wall collisions, median zero, but 100.0\% boundary-adjacent cell occupancy across 20 deterministic $200{\times}200$/$N{=}20$ episodes) confirm are heuristics of the flat-MLP inductive bias rather than set-structured reasoning over the sensor field. A permutation-invariant Relational RL policy, trained with PPO on a per-sensor encoder with masked sum-pooling, resolves the diagnostic: boundary-cell occupancy falls from 100.0\% to 21.5\% (mean), wall collisions drop to zero across all 20 diagnostic seeds, and the policy leads the DQN at every multi-seed condition. NDR at $300{\times}300$/$N{=}30$ rises from 52.7\% to 97.7\%, and at $500{\times}500$/$N{=}50$ from 13.0\% to 56.4\% (all $p < 0.001$, Welch's $t$-test). The comparison changes both the policy architecture and the training algorithm at once; separating their individual contributions requires a further controlled ablation, noted in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.
