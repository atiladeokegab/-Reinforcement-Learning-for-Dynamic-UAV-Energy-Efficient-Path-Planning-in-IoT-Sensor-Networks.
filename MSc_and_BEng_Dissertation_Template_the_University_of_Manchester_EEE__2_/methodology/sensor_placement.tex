\subsection{Sensor Placement and Reward}

Figure~\ref{fig:system_model} illustrates the complete system model
for the $500\times 500$ evaluation condition. Sensor coordinates are
sampled from $\mathcal{U}(0, L)\times\mathcal{U}(0, W)$, modelling an
unstructured ad-hoc topology. This ensures the agent learns a
generalisable policy rather than memorising a fixed map, and
naturally creates density clusters that induce poor SF Allocation.

\subsubsection{Multi-objective reward}
\label{sec:reward}

A dense reward avoids the vanishing-gradient problem of sparse mission-success objectives. The total reward at step $t$ is
\begin{equation}
    R_t = w_{\mathrm{coll}}\,R_{\mathrm{coll}}
        - w_{\mathrm{energy}}\,C_{\mathrm{energy}}
        - w_{\mathrm{AoI}}\,C_{\mathrm{AoI}}
        - w_{\mathrm{hover}}\,C_{\mathrm{hover}}
    \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
with four components. $R_{\mathrm{coll}}$ rewards bytes collected, scaled by per-sensor urgency: a high-buffer sensor yields more reward than one recently drained. $C_{\mathrm{energy}}$ penalises each action in proportion to its power draw, which biases the policy toward efficient trajectories. $C_{\mathrm{AoI}}$ penalises mean Age of Information across the sensor field~\cite{kaul2012realtime,abdelmagid2019aoi}; without it the policy concentrates on a single high-yield cluster rather than spreading flight time. $C_{\mathrm{hover}}$ penalises hovering at a sensor whose buffer is empty, so the policy does not waste steps after collection is done. All components are listed with calibrated weights in Table~\ref{tab:reward_components}; no single component exceeds 15\% of total episode reward (Appendix~\ref{app:reward_scale}).
