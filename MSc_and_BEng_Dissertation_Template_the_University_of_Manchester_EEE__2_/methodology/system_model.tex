\subsection{System Model}

We consider a remote IoT monitoring environment comprising a set \(\mathcal{N} = \{1, \ldots, N\}\)
of static sensor devices uniformly distributed over a rectangular Region of Interest (RoI)
\(\mathcal{W} \subset \mathbb{R}^2\). These devices continuously generate monitoring data,
which accumulates in finite transmission buffers. Unlike simple deadline-based models, we
assume data carries value only if it is collected before the buffer capacity \(B_{\max}\) is
exceeded, after which packet loss occurs.

A rotary-wing UAV is the mobile LoRaWAN gateway, dispatched to harvest data from the
ground sensors. The mission duration is fixed to a finite horizon \(T\) and divided into
discrete time steps \(t = 0, 1, \ldots, T\), each of duration \(\Delta t\). The UAV flies
at fixed altitude \(h\) to maintain a consistent ground-to-air channel geometry, updating
its horizontal position \(p_t = (x_t, y_t)\) at each step. The system employs the LoRaWAN
protocol with orthogonal Spreading Factors (SF), enabling simultaneous uploads from multiple
sensors when they are assigned distinct SFs by the Adaptive Data Rate (ADR) mechanism. The
UAV must therefore optimise its trajectory to actively manage spectral assignments and
prevent network congestion.

\noindent\textbf{Assumption 1.} \textit{The UAV operates at fixed altitude $h$, reducing
trajectory optimisation to a 2D horizontal problem; vertical dynamics are abstracted into
fixed power profiles $P_{\text{hover}}$ and $P_{\text{flight}}$.}

\noindent\textbf{Assumption 2.} \textit{The LoRaWAN gateway is single-channel multi-SF:
at most one transmission per SF is decoded simultaneously; intra-SF collisions are resolved
via the Capture Effect (Section~\ref{sec:capture}).}

\noindent\textbf{Assumption 3.} \textit{Sensors are LoRaWAN Class~A devices with a 10\%
duty cycle\footnote{The EU 868\,MHz ISM band mandates a 1\% radio duty cycle. The 10\%
parameter models the \emph{effective} per-timestep transmission probability (wake-up
rate $\times$ packet frequency $\times$ channel access). Sensitivity is tested down to
4\% in Section~\ref{sec:robustness}.} and Poisson data generation; network-initiated wake-up is
infeasible, so the UAV must arrive during a node's natural transmission window.}

\noindent\textbf{Assumption 4.} \textit{The UAV retains a 20\% battery reserve
($0.20 \times E_{\max} = 54.8$\,Wh) for safe return-to-base. The episode terminates
when remaining energy reaches this threshold. Upon landing, a fully charged relay UAV
is immediately dispatched to continue the mission; continuous area coverage is therefore
maintained by a two-UAV rotation, and each agent is evaluated on its performance within
a single flight.}

The relay-handover model is operationally realistic: commercial LoRaWAN UAV deployments
(e.g.\ DJI TB60 with a charging dock) use automatic battery-swap or relay dispatch to
sustain continuous data harvesting over long missions~\cite{mozaffari2019tutorial}. The
effective episode energy budget is therefore $E_{\text{eff}} = 0.80 \times 274 = 219.2$\,Wh,
reducing the maximum flight steps proportionally. This tightens the feasibility constraints
derived in Section~\ref{sec:link_budget}: the maximum traversable distance in a single
episode becomes $d_{\max} \approx 15{,}782$\,m, making full coverage at $1{,}000\times1{,}000$
scale even more infeasible (TSP tour $\approx 44{,}700$\,m, a factor of 2.8 beyond
$d_{\max}$).

\subsubsection{UAV Kinematics and Energy Consumption}

We consider a rotary-wing Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) operating within a discretised 3D
grid space \(\mathcal{W} \subset \mathbb{R}^3\). The mission is defined over a finite time
horizon \(T\), discretised into time steps of duration \(\Delta t\). At each time step \(k\),
the UAV's kinematic state is defined by its 3D position \(\mathbf{p}_k = [x_k, y_k, z_k]^T\).

The UAV is controlled by a discrete action space \(\mathcal{A}\) consisting of five high-level
motion primitives:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A} = \{\text{MoveN},\ \text{MoveS},\ \text{MoveE},\ \text{MoveW},\ \text{Hover}\}
    \label{eq:action_space}
\end{equation}
Each movement action updates the UAV's position by a fixed step size \(\delta_d\) in the
corresponding cardinal direction. The Hover action maintains the current position
\(\mathbf{p}_{k+1} = \mathbf{p}_k\), which is critical for stabilising the LoRaWAN link to
facilitate data packet reception.

The energy consumption model is state-dependent. Let \(E_k\) denote the remaining battery
energy at step \(k\). The energy evolution is governed by:
\begin{equation}
    E_{k+1} = E_k - P(a_k) \cdot \Delta t \label{eq:energy_evolution}
\end{equation}
where \(P(a_k)\) is the instantaneous power consumption. We define distinct power profiles
for flight modes:
\begin{equation}
    P(a_k) =
    \begin{cases}
        P_{\text{hover}}  & \text{if } a_k = \text{Hover} \\
        P_{\text{flight}} & \text{if } a_k \in \{\text{Move}\}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:power_model}
\end{equation}
Consistent with rotary-wing aerodynamics, we model the power consumption such that
\(P_{\text{hover}} > P_{\text{flight}}\) due to the lack of translational lift in static
flight.\footnote{In rotary-wing UAVs, translational lift (the additional lift gained from
forward flight) means that hovering requires more power than forward flight at moderate
speeds.} Accordingly, we assign a higher power cost to the Hover action (\SI{700}{W})
compared to translational Move actions (\SI{500}{W}). This power differential incentivises
the agent to optimise for efficient flight trajectories and penalises excessive loitering,
thereby aligning the reward signal with the physical reality of limited onboard energy
storage.

\subsubsection{Channel Model and Protocol Modelling}

The ground-to-air channel is modelled using the Two-Ray Ground Reflection
model~\cite{rappaport2002wireless}, which is appropriate for low-altitude UAV platforms where
the direct LoS path and ground-reflected path interfere. The received power is:
\begin{equation}
    P_r = P_t G_t G_r \frac{h_t^2 h_r^2}{d^4} \label{eq:tworay}
\end{equation}
where $h_t$, $h_r$ are transmitter and receiver heights, $d$ is the 3D slant range, and $G_t$, $G_r$
are antenna gains. The quartic decay ($1/d^4$) enforces realistic data-collection radius constraints
and means small changes in the UAV's position have a magnified impact on RSSI, a property
that the DRL agent must learn to exploit~\cite{alhourani2014optimal}.

\subsubsection{Device Class and Traffic Model}

Sensor nodes are modelled as LoRaWAN Class~A devices~\cite{augustin2016lora}, operating
asynchronously with an effective 10\% per-timestep transmission probability (see
Assumption~3 footnote). Data generation follows a Poisson process with rate $\lambda_i$ per
sensor, accumulating in finite buffers of capacity $B_{\max}$. Once a buffer is full, new
packets are discarded, creating a hard collection deadline for the agent. This models
realistic energy-harvesting deployments where network-initiated wake-up is infeasible.

\paragraph{Propagation delay and ADR convergence latency.}
Radio propagation delay over the maximum collection range of 3{,}650\,m is
$\tau_{\text{prop}} = d/c \approx 12\,\mu$s, six orders of magnitude below the
1-second timestep duration and therefore not modelled. The dominant latency in the
system is instead \emph{ADR convergence}: when the UAV repositions to reduce path
loss, the EMA filter ($\hat{\text{RSSI}}_{t+1} = (1-\lambda)\hat{\text{RSSI}}_t + \lambda\,\text{RSSI}_t$, $\lambda = 0.1$) requires approximately
$1/\lambda = 10$ timesteps ($\sim$10\,s) before the SF assignment falls to the
lower, higher-throughput value. This inertia is intentional: it prevents SF from
oscillating due to transient shadowing and forces the agent to commit to a position
long enough for ADR to converge before flying elsewhere.

\subsubsection{Collision Resolution via Capture Effect} \label{sec:capture}

Intra-SF collisions (multiple sensors on the same spreading factor) are resolved using the
Capture Effect~\cite{croce2020lora}: the gateway decodes sensor $i$ if its received power
exceeds the interference from all co-channel sensors by the capture threshold $\tau_{\text{cap}} = \SI{6}{dB}$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{P_{r,i}}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_{\text{co-SF}}} P_{r,j} + N_0} \geq \tau_{\text{cap}}
    \label{eq:capture}
\end{equation}
This forces the agent to optimise its trajectory not merely for minimum RSSI coverage but to
induce the power differential required for successful capture during congestion events, a
spatial optimisation that greedy heuristics cannot plan for. For two sensors colliding on the
same SF, the capture condition simplifies to $d_{j,t}^4 / d_{i,t}^4 \geq \tau_{\text{cap}}$,
requiring the UAV to position itself strictly closer to the desired sensor.

\paragraph{Timestep-level collision model and SF independence.}
A key question is whether an ongoing SF reception can be interrupted by a later-arriving
transmission on the same SF. In real LoRa hardware, a late-arriving co-SF signal can
corrupt an already-locked packet if it exceeds the noise floor; however, transmissions on
\emph{different} SFs are fully orthogonal and never interrupt one another.

The simulation resolves collisions at the \emph{timestep} level rather than the
packet-timing level, which is standard practice in LoRaWAN system
simulation~\cite{augustin2016lora,croce2020lora}. At each collect step the procedure is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Every in-range sensor with buffered data independently decides to transmit with
    probability $P_{\text{overall}} = P_{\text{link}} \times P_{\text{cycle}}$, where
    $P_{\text{link}} = \sigma(\text{SNR}_{\text{dB}} - \text{SNR}_{\text{req}}(\text{SF}))$
    is a sigmoid link-margin probability and $P_{\text{cycle}} = 0.10$ is the duty-cycle
    probability.
    \item Sensors are grouped by their current SF. Each SF group is an independent
    channel, transmissions across different SF groups never collide.
    \item Within each SF group, if exactly one sensor transmits: it is decoded
    unconditionally. If two or more transmit simultaneously: the Capture Effect criterion
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:capture}) is applied. If the strongest signal exceeds all interference
    by $\tau_{\text{cap}} = \SI{6}{dB}$, it is decoded; otherwise destructive
    interference destroys all packets in that group.
    \item Data is collected from every SF group that produced a winner, up to one
    decoded packet per SF per timestep.
\end{enumerate}
This four-phase model means the maximum concurrent throughput per timestep is
$|\mathcal{S}| = 4$ packets (one per SF in $\{7, 9, 11, 12\}$), achieved only when
the UAV has positioned itself such that sensors are distributed across all four SFs
,  precisely the condition the DQN learns to engineer.

\subsubsection{Gateway Architecture}
\label{sec:gateway}

The UAV acts as a single-channel, multi-SF LoRaWAN gateway. It is important to be precise
about what this means, as it is central to both the problem and the DQN's learned strategy.

\paragraph{One channel, one device per SF, one transmission at a time per SF.}
The gateway operates on a single radio frequency channel. Within that channel, LoRa
Spreading Factors are \emph{quasi-orthogonal}: transmissions on different SFs occupy the
same bandwidth but are distinguishable by the gateway's correlator bank because each SF
uses a different chirp rate~\cite{augustin2016lora}. Consequently, the gateway can
decode \emph{one uplink packet per SF simultaneously}, up to four concurrent
receptions across $\mathcal{S} = \{7, 9, 11, 12\}$. This is not multi-channel reception;
it is the inherent spectral orthogonality of LoRa modulation exploited by a single radio.

\paragraph{Class~A device constraint.}
All sensors are LoRaWAN Class~A devices~\cite{augustin2016lora}. Class~A is the most
restrictive device class: a sensor opens its two downlink receive windows only
\emph{immediately after} completing a self-initiated uplink. At all other times the
radio is off. This has two direct consequences for the UAV mission:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No network-initiated wake-up.} The UAV gateway cannot command a sensor
    to transmit. It must be within collection range \emph{at the moment} a sensor
    naturally opens its uplink window. The 10\% effective duty-cycle probability models
    this constraint: at each timestep, each in-range sensor independently transmits with
    probability $P_{\text{cycle}} = 0.10$.
    \item \textbf{Uplink-limited throughput.} Data rate is bounded by the sensor's SF
    and duty-cycle window. A sensor on SF12 (250 bits/s effective) transfers far fewer
    bytes per window than a sensor on SF7 (5470 bits/s effective), so inducing ADR
    convergence to lower SFs directly increases bytes collected per visit.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Why SF diversity matters.}
Because the gateway decodes one device per SF, having $K$ sensors within range on $K$
\emph{distinct} SFs yields $K$ simultaneous decodable transmissions per step. If all $K$
sensors share the same SF (SF Monoculture), at most one is decoded (via Capture Effect)
and the remaining $K-1$ transmissions are lost. The UAV's positioning therefore controls
not just link quality (RSSI $\to$ SF assignment via ADR) but the number of concurrent
receptions, making trajectory planning a direct lever on network throughput. This is the
mechanism the DQN learns to exploit and that one-step greedy heuristics cannot plan for.

\subsubsection{Link Budget, Collection Radius, and Operational Envelope}
\label{sec:link_budget}

A central design choice is the UAV operating altitude $h_r = 100$\,m. This is the
regulatory ceiling for uncontrolled airspace operations under EU and UK drone legislation
(the ``open category''), allowing deployment without special authorisation. The DJI TB60,
on which the energy model is based, is rated for operations up to 120\,m AGL. At 100\,m
altitude the Two-Ray model applies: the direct LoS path and ground-reflected path
interfere constructively, producing the $1/d^4$ decay of Eq.~(\ref{eq:tworay}). Each
grid unit represents 10\,m of ground distance.

\paragraph{Deriving collection radii.}
Substituting the simulation parameters ($P_t = 14$\,dBm, $G_t = G_r = 0$\,dBi,
$h_t = 1.5$\,m, $h_r = 100$\,m) into the Two-Ray received-power equation and inverting
for horizontal range $r$ (the ground-projected distance from the sensor to the UAV
ground-track) yields:

\begin{equation}
    r = \left(10^{\,\frac{P_t + 20\log_{10}(h_t h_r) - \text{RSSI}_{\min}}{40}}\right)
    \label{eq:range}
\end{equation}

The ADR mechanism maps RSSI to Spreading Factor; the gateway can only decode a
transmission if RSSI exceeds the SF-specific demodulation floor (Table~\ref{tab:sf_range}).

\paragraph{Justification of evaluation grid scales.}
The grid scales are chosen to span qualitatively distinct regimes defined by the
interaction between $r_{\max} = 365$ grid units and the grid geometry
(Table~\ref{tab:grid_regimes}).

\label{tab:grid_regimes}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}

\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

At $100\times100$ the maximum corner-to-corner distance (141 units) is smaller than
$r_{\max}$ (365 units), so a sensor is always within collection range regardless of UAV
position: navigation has no link-budget effect and the problem reduces to SF diversity
management alone. At $500\times500$ the diagonal (707 units) is nearly double $r_{\max}$,
meaning sensors near opposite corners are mutually unreachable without navigation.

The energy-feasibility bound is derived from the battery model. With $E_{\max} = 274$\,Wh
and flight power $P_{\text{flight}} = 500$\,W per step ($\Delta t = 1$\,s per step), the
maximum traversable distance is:
\begin{equation}
    d_{\max} = \frac{E_{\max}}{P_{\text{flight}} \cdot \Delta t} \times \delta_d
    = \frac{274\,\text{Wh} \times 3600\,\text{s/h}}{500\,\text{W} \times 1\,\text{s}}
    \times 10\,\text{m} = 19{,}728\,\text{m}
    \label{eq:dmax}
\end{equation}

For $N = 20$ sensors on a $1{,}000\times1{,}000$ grid, the estimated TSP tour length of
44{,}700\,m exceeds $d_{\max} = 19{,}728$\,m by a factor of 2.3: no agent, regardless
of policy quality, can visit all sensors within the energy budget. This establishes the
$1{,}000\times1{,}000$ grid as a \emph{physically infeasible} condition and the
$500\times500$ grid as the upper boundary of the single-UAV operational envelope. These
four grid scales therefore systematically probe from trivially solvable to physically
impossible, enabling a principled characterisation of where protocol-aware navigation
adds value.
