\subsection{LoRaWAN Physical Layer}

LoRa (Long Range) is a proprietary spread-spectrum modulation technique derived from Chirp
Spread Spectrum (CSS). It encodes information by varying the frequency of ``chirps'' over
time, which provides exceptional resilience to interference and noise~\cite{augustin2016lora,bor2016lora}.

\subsubsection{Spreading Factors and Orthogonality}

The key parameter in LoRa modulation is the Spreading Factor (SF), which determines the
duration of a chirp. LoRaWAN defines six orthogonal spreading factors,
\(\text{SF} \in \{7, 8, \ldots, 12\}\).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SF7}: Lowest range, highest data rate, shortest Time-on-Air.
    \item \textbf{SF12}: Longest range, lowest data rate, longest Time-on-Air.
\end{itemize}

A defining feature of LoRa is the orthogonality of these spreading factors. Signals modulated
with different SFs can theoretically be received simultaneously by a gateway on the same
frequency channel without interfering with each other~\cite{augustin2016lora,reynders2016range}. This
property creates the ``concurrent channel capacity'' that this project aims to exploit.

\subsubsection{The Capture Effect}

In dense networks, collisions occur when two devices transmit on the same SF and frequency
simultaneously. However, LoRa receivers exhibit the Capture Effect: if one signal is
sufficiently stronger than the interfering signal (typically by a margin of \SI{6}{dB}), the
receiver can successfully decode the stronger packet while suppressing the weaker
one~\cite{croce2020lora,zorbas2025revisiting}. This physical phenomenon is modelled in the simulation to
resolve co-SF collisions.
