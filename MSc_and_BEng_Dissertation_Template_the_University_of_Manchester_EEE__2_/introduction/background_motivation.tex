\subsection{Background and motivation}
IoT sensor networks support smart agriculture, environmental
monitoring and disaster response~\cite{wei2022uav} in settings
where fixed infrastructure is unavailable or prohibitively expensive.
UAVs provide on-demand data collection as mobile
gateways~\cite{wei2022uav,mozaffari2019tutorial,zeng2016wireless},
and combining their mobility with the long-range, low-power LoRaWAN
protocol~\cite{augustin2016lora,bor2016lora} allows coverage of vast
sensor fields with minimal infrastructure~\cite{nakamura2022lora}.

Integrating mobile UAVs into dense LoRaWAN networks introduces
challenges that standard protocols cannot address. The LoRaWAN
Adaptive Data Rate mechanism is engineered for static,
ground-based networks. In dense deployments where link qualities
change rapidly, Zorbas et al.~\cite{zorbas2025revisiting} show that
standard ADR collapses: devices converge to the same Spreading
Factor, producing intra-SF collisions and measurable degradation of
delivery ratio. This SF Allocation wastes the network's concurrent
channel capacity. Standard path-planning heuristics optimise solely
for flight distance and ignore the radio
protocol~\cite{xiong2023flyinglora}, while mathematical optimisation
approaches solve allocation on static networks but lack the online
adaptability required for mobile
gateways~\cite{zorbas2025revisiting}. Several independent lines of work~\cite{zorbas2025revisiting,nguyen20226g,luong2019drl,challita2018interference}
point toward reinforcement learning for this class of dynamically
evolving channel: RL adapts online without stationary channel
assumptions, and the UAV's position becomes a direct control input
for the protocol.

A reinforcement-learning framework is only as good as the inductive
bias of the policy network it is trained with. The UAV-LoRaWAN
problem presents an unordered set of sensors whose routing cost
depends on inter-sensor geometry, and whose protocol state evolves
independently per sensor. A flat multilayer-perceptron policy,
consuming the concatenation of the UAV state and sensor feature
vectors, has no explicit mechanism for representing this set
structure. The flat-MLP policy has a hard architectural ceiling. Better training will not raise it. The problem is the observation encoding.
The empirical evidence takes the form of a representational
diagnostic (Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic}): the trained DQN spends
$100\%$ of flight time on boundary-adjacent cells despite the
boundary being explicitly penalised, because perimeter-adjacent
flight is the simplest stable trajectory the flat MLP can express
given the observation encoding. A permutation-invariant policy
resolves the behaviour under matched training conditions
(Section~\ref{sec:relational}).
