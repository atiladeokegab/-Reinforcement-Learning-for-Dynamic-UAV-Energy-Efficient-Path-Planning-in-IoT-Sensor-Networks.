\subsection{Experimental Setup}

All agents are evaluated on the high-fidelity Gymnasium simulation with the UAV flying at
a fixed altitude of 100\,m and each grid unit equal to 10\,m. The battery capacity is
274\,Wh (modelled on the DJI TB60) and the episode horizon is 2{,}100 timesteps. The DQN
policy is deterministic at test time ($\varepsilon = 0$) and uses the zero-padded
observation vector ($N_{\max} = 50$) as during training, with frame stacking $k = 4$.

\paragraph{Evaluation Sweeps.}
Sweep A varies sensor count $N \in \{10, 20, 30, 40\}$ on a fixed $500\times500$ grid.
Sweep B varies grid size $\{100\times100,\, 300\times300,\, 500\times500,\, 1{,}000\times1{,}000\}$
with fixed $N=20$. Each condition uses twenty independent random seeds; all three agents
are compared on identical sensor layouts.

\paragraph{Baseline Agents.}
\textbf{Nearest Greedy} moves to the closest sensor with buffered data at each step.
\textbf{SF-Aware Greedy} scores candidates as
$\text{Score}_i = (13 - \text{SF}_i)\cdot w_1 - d_i\cdot w_2$,
prioritising SF7--SF9 sensors for higher instantaneous throughput.
A random-walk agent was not included as a formal baseline because both greedy heuristics
already achieve 100\% sensor coverage and competitive throughput; the relevant comparison is
whether the DQN's learned policy outperforms \emph{competent} planning strategies, not
whether it outperforms undirected exploration.

\paragraph{Evaluation Metrics.}
Primary metrics are cumulative reward, network coverage (fraction of sensors visited),
Jain's Fairness Index~\cite{jain1984quantitative}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{J} = \frac{\bigl(\sum_{i} r_i\bigr)^2}{N \sum_{i} r_i^2}
    \label{eq:jains}
\end{equation}
and energy efficiency (bytes/Wh). Statistical significance is assessed via Welch's
$t$-test (two-sided, $\alpha = 0.05$) on the twenty-seed reward distributions.
