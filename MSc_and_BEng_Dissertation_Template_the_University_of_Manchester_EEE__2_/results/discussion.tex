\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The multi-condition sweep reveals three regimes defined by the interaction between UAV
energy budget, link budget, and sensor density, each with a distinct implication for
policy design.

\subsubsection{SF-Aware Greedy as the Dominant Heuristic}

At the feasible scale (300$\times$300) and marginal scale (500$\times$500), the SF-Aware
Greedy baseline consistently outperforms the DQN on both coverage rate and Jain's fairness
index. The advantage is statistically significant at 500$\times$500 ($d = -1.84$,
$p = 0.039$). The mechanism is straightforward: SF-Aware Greedy hard-codes the key
protocol insight, route toward the sensor with the lowest current SF assignment , 
which maximises both bytes per visit (lower SF = higher data rate) and SF diversity across
simultaneously in-range sensors. The DQN, trained with a multi-objective reward that does
not directly incentivise SF diversity, learns a policy closer to Nearest-Sensor Greedy
than to SF-Aware Greedy at the scales evaluated.

This result does not invalidate the DRL approach; rather, it identifies what the learned
policy is missing: an explicit representation of sensor topology and SF assignments.
A greedy heuristic encodes domain knowledge that the DQN must infer from delayed reward
signals over $3 \times 10^6$ training steps with only a local 2D position and AoI state
representation. The attention-based and graph-neural-network extensions
(Section~\ref{sec:future_work}) are specifically designed to close this gap by providing
the agent with a structured relational view of the sensor field.

\subsubsection{What the DQN Learns}

Despite not outperforming SF-Aware Greedy, the DQN exhibits a learned behaviour not
present in either greedy baseline: it sustains proximity to sensor clusters for
approximately $1/\lambda = 10$ consecutive steps before repositioning, allowing the
EMA-ADR filter to converge to a lower SF before the agent moves away. This
\emph{dwell-then-reposition} pattern is the temporal structure required to exploit
ADR convergence as a delayed reward, and it is structurally inaccessible to a one-step
greedy policy whose look-ahead horizon is $h = 1$. The DQN's effective planning horizon
($1/(1-\gamma) = 100$ steps with $\gamma = 0.99$) is sufficient to learn this strategy.

The pattern is observable in single-episode traces (Section~\ref{sec:single_episode}) but
does not translate into a aggregate coverage advantage at 500$\times$500 because the UAV
visits too few sensors in total for the EMA-ADR benefit to accumulate into a measurable
throughput difference. In the feasible regime (300$\times$300), SFGreedy's more direct
routing covers more sensors per episode, offsetting any per-visit throughput advantage the
DQN might gain from dwell behaviour.

\subsubsection{Operational Envelope as the Primary Finding}

The most robust finding from the sweep is the operational envelope characterisation
(Table~\ref{tab:grid_summary}). The performance of \emph{all three agents} collapses
above 500$\times$500: NDR drops below 12\% for every agent at $1{,}000\times1{,}000$.
This is not a consequence of policy quality but of physical infeasibility: the $d_{\max}
\approx 15{,}782$\,m achievable in a single episode is insufficient to traverse the TSP
tour ($\approx 44{,}700$\,m) required to visit all 20 sensors. No learned or heuristic
single-UAV policy can overcome this constraint; the operational boundary is set by the
link budget and energy capacity, not by the algorithm.

This boundary is a novel contribution: the prior literature reports DRL results on
fixed grid sizes without characterising where physical feasibility ends
\cite{mozaffari2019tutorial,li2020uav,liu2019uav}. Identifying the 300$\times$300
boundary as the effective single-UAV operational limit for the DJI TB60 / LoRaWAN Class~A
configuration directly informs deployment planning and motivates the multi-UAV extension
as the highest-priority future direction.

\subsubsection{Limitations}

Three limitations bound the scope of these conclusions.

\textbf{Training budget and reward engineering.} The DQN was trained for $3 \times 10^6$
steps across 16 conditions. Two reward variants were evaluated to test whether the
SFGreedy gap is a reward-engineering problem rather than an architectural one.
Variant~A (\texttt{penalty\_battery}$=0$) removes the battery penalty, confirming it
was a near-zero dead term ($<0.1\%$ of episode reward). Variant~B additionally raises
the movement bonus to $+200$ per step (versus $+10$ in the baseline), making
deliberate repositioning worth approximately one-third of a mediocre SF11 collection
per step. Both variants produce statistically indistinguishable results from the
baseline DQN at 500$\times$500 ($|\Delta\mathcal{J}| < 0.015$, $n = 5$ seeds). This
null result is informative: the performance gap is \emph{not} addressable by reward
shaping alone. The MLP policy lacks the structured relational encoding needed to
implement an SF-directional routing strategy regardless of how movement is incentivised.
The A2 ablation (instant ADR, $\lambda = 1.0$) establishes an upper bound: removing ADR
latency yields $+4.1\%$ mean reward, confirming that the EMA filter is a real planning
cost that SFGreedy partially avoids by routing toward already-low-SF sensors.

\textbf{Simulation fidelity.} The channel model assumes a flat ground plane; real terrain
introduces additional shadowing. All evaluation is simulation-based and the joint effect
of simultaneous GPS error, path-loss mismatch, and wind drift remains unquantified (see
Section~\ref{sec:robustness} for independent perturbation analysis).

\textbf{Action space.} The five-action 2D discrete action space cannot express altitude
control or diagonal movement. SFGreedy can route diagonally in Euclidean space while the
DQN is constrained to cardinal steps, creating an inherent path-efficiency disadvantage.
Continuous or altitude-augmented action spaces (Section~\ref{sec:future_work}) would
reduce this gap.
