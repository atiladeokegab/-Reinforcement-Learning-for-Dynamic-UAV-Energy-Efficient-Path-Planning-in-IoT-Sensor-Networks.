\subsection{Discussion}

The results confirm the central hypothesis of this work: a protocol-aware DRL agent
trained with domain randomisation and curriculum learning outperforms standard
path-planning heuristics across a range of network densities, with the advantage
growing as the problem becomes more challenging.

The DQN policy's key advantage is its ability to learn implicit positioning rules that
maximise concurrent transmission across orthogonal spreading factors---behaviour that
the greedy baselines cannot replicate because they never reposition. Per-step action
analysis confirms that greedy agents issue a collection action at every timestep (100\%
hover fraction), whereas the DQN devotes 2.9\% of steps to deliberate movement
($p = 0.002$, Welch's $t$-test). The DQN's collection steps yield $48.7$\,B on average
versus $42.7$\,B for SF-Aware Greedy (+14\%), though this advantage does not reach
statistical significance at $n = 5$ seeds ($p = 0.209$). Capture Effect trigger counts
are statistically indistinguishable across agents ($p = 1.0$), confirming that the
DQN's throughput gain is driven by improved spatial positioning for multi-SF concurrent
collection, not by resolving a greater number of intra-SF collisions.
At low sensor counts ($N = 10$), this advantage is small because the routing problem
itself is easy and both approaches achieve near-perfect performance. At high sensor
counts ($N = 40$), the learned policy delivers measurably higher throughput
(+7.4\%), higher fairness (+3.9\%), and higher energy efficiency (+7.3\%).

Several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the high variance of the DQN results
at $N \geq 20$ indicates that the policy is sensitive to the spatial layout of sensors.
Layouts where sensor clusters naturally span multiple spreading factors favour the DQN;
uniformly distributed layouts reduce its advantage. Second, the fairness metric degrades substantially at high density for all agents, a
consequence of the fixed battery budget and single-UAV constraint rather than of the
algorithm itself---a fundamental limitation also observed in UAV-assisted mmWave coverage
scenarios~\cite{jin2023equalizing}. Third, all experiments
are conducted in simulation; the physics model, while high-fidelity, does not capture
real-world effects such as wind disturbance, GPS error, or hardware communication
latency. These limitations are discussed further in Chapter~\ref{sec:conclusions}.
