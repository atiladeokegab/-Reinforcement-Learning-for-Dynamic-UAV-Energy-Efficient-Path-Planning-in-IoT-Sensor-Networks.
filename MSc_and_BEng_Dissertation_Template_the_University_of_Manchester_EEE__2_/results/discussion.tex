\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The DQN's flat-MLP encoder creates an architectural ceiling: perimeter-adjacent flight is the most stable trajectory it can express given the observation encoding, and once this ceiling binds at $N \ge 30$, protocol-blind greedy baselines outperform the trained agent (Table~\ref{tab:scalability_ndr}). Replacing the flat trunk with a permutation-invariant Deep-Sets encoder closes most of this gap at every condition beyond $N{=}20$. The ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) confirms that protocol components (A1, A2) add negligible inference-time value, while AoI features (A3) are load-bearing at every scale; the A4 result and the reward-supplement control both confirm the Relational-RL gain is architectural rather than reward-driven.

The comparison changes both representation and algorithm at once, so the NDR gap cannot be attributed to either alone. Relational RL uses PPO rather than DQN, so on-policy vs off-policy training is also confounded with the encoder change. The reward-supplement control rules out the reward channel, but the definitive isolation, training a flat-MLP with PPO, remains as future work (Section~\ref{sec:future_work}). The gain is clear regardless: switching to a permutation-invariant encoder under otherwise identical conditions produces a large, reproducible improvement that grows with task difficulty.
