\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results confirm the central hypothesis: a protocol-aware DRL agent trained with domain
randomisation and curriculum learning outperforms standard path-planning heuristics across a
range of network densities, with the advantage growing as the problem becomes harder.

\subsubsection{Mechanism of the DQN Advantage}

The DQN's key advantage is learning positioning rules that maximise concurrent orthogonal-SF
transmission. Greedy agents hover at every step (100\% hover fraction), whereas the DQN devotes
2.9\% of steps to deliberate movement ($p = 0.002$, $d = 2.1$, large), yielding $48.7$\,B per
collection step vs.\ $42.7$\,B for SF-Aware Greedy (+14\%). Capture Effect trigger counts are
indistinguishable ($p = 1.0$), confirming the gain is from positioning, not collision resolution.

The SF dynamics analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:sf_dynamics} in Appendix~\ref{app:additional_figures}) provides the causal explanation:
by maintaining proximity for multiple steps, the DQN allows the EMA-ADR filter to converge
to lower SF assignments before repositioning. This converts ADR latency---normally a fidelity
cost---into a feature: induced SF downgrades enable high-throughput orthogonal-SF concurrent
collection that greedy policies cannot plan for.

\subsubsection{Density Dependence and Effect Sizes}

All four density conditions reach statistical significance ($p < 0.05$, Table~\ref{tab:effect_sizes}). Two competing forces shape the pattern: as density grows, the SF-diversity mechanism becomes more impactful (mean advantage rises from $+2.3\%$ at $N=10$ to $+9.0\%$ at $N=30$), but spatial layout variance also scales with density (std = 1.120M at $N=40$ vs.\ 0.040M at $N=10$), diluting the effect size. This explains the non-monotonic $d$ pattern ($d=2.52 \to 0.78 \to 0.54$): high-density performance is more sensitive to spatial arrangement.

The same trend holds across all three metrics: the efficiency advantage grows from $+2.3$\,B/Wh at $N=10$ to $+20.7$\,B/Wh at $N=40$, confirming the reward advantage is not a reward-function artefact but reflects real data collection gain. The Jain's Index advantage at $N=40$ ($+4.2\%$, $d=0.40$) confirms that the throughput gain does not come at the cost of fairness — the AoI penalty continues to distribute attention across sensors even in the high-variance density regime. \subsubsection{Limitations}

Three limitations bound the scope of the conclusions. First, the throughput advantage ($+2.3\%$--$+9.0\%$) is
statistically significant but operationally modest. The A2 ablation (instant ADR, $\lambda=1.0$, $+7.8\%$
reward) establishes an approximate upper bound on any positioning strategy's achievable gain; the DQN
recovers roughly half of this via deliberate repositioning, indicating the SF Monoculture is partially
self-limiting due to the 6\,dB Capture Effect threshold.

Second, the channel model assumes a flat ground plane and a single-channel gateway; real deployments
involve terrain-induced shadowing beyond the tested $\Delta n=+1.0$ perturbation space, and multi-channel
gateways would reduce the relative cost of intra-SF collisions by allowing concurrent reception across
multiple frequency channels---diminishing the SF Monoculture pressure that the DQN's repositioning
strategy is designed to exploit. All evaluation is simulation-based; the
joint effect of simultaneous real-world perturbations remains unquantified.

Third, Jain's fairness at $N=40$ does not reach significance ($p=0.129$) because the fixed battery
makes equitable coverage geometrically infeasible at high density~\cite{jin2023equalizing}. The
five-action 2D action space further constrains positioning; altitude control and continuous actions
remain as extensions (Section~\ref{sec:future_work}).
