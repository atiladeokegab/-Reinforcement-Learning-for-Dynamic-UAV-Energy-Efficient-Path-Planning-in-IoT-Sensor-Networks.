\subsection{Scalability Analysis}

\subsubsection{Sensor-Count Scalability}

Figure~\ref{fig:scalability} shows the DQN advantage in cumulative reward and Jain's
fairness index as a function of sensor count ($N \in \{10, 20, 30, 40\}$, grid fixed at
$500 \times 500$). Both metrics exhibit a consistent positive advantage for the DQN that
increases with $N$, confirming that the benefit of a learned policy over hand-crafted
heuristics is most pronounced in denser, more challenging networks. The reward advantage
grows from $+2.7\%$ at $N = 10$ to $+7.4\%$ at $N = 40$ against SF-Aware Greedy, and
the fairness advantage grows from near-zero to $+3.9\%$.

\noindent The concave shape of the reward-advantage curve---larger gains at $N = 20$
and $N = 30$ than at $N = 40$---reflects the interaction between sensor density and the
SF orthogonality constraint. At $N = 40$ on a $500 \times 500$ grid, the network is
dense enough that inter-sensor distances become smaller than the UAV's effective collection
radius for SF7, leading to frequent Capture Effect events that all agents partially exploit.
The DQN's advantage therefore saturates slightly relative to the peak at $N = 30$.

\subsubsection{Grid-Size Scalability}

A second sweep varied the operational area from $100 \times 100$ to $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$
cells (corresponding to $1$\,km$^2$ to $100$\,km$^2$ in the physical simulation, with each
grid unit equal to $10$\,m and UAV altitude fixed at $100$\,m), with $N = 20$ sensors and
five random seeds per condition. This sweep spans three qualitatively different LoRa
operating regimes: at $100 \times 100$ most sensors fall within SF7 range; at
$500 \times 500$ the dominant SFs are SF9--SF11; and at $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ the
network operates predominantly in the SF11/SF12 regime where ADR latency is most severe.

index for all three agents across the four grid sizes.

\noindent Four observations merit discussion.

\paragraph{Compact grids (SF7 regime).}
At $100 \times 100$ and $300 \times 300$, all agents achieve near-perfect fairness
($\mathcal{J} > 0.999$) as the compact area keeps all sensors within SF7 range, almost
eliminating the monoculture risk. Interestingly, the SF-Aware Greedy achieves a
marginally \emph{higher} Jain's Index than the DQN at $300 \times 300$ ($0.9996$ versus
$0.9988$), while the DQN achieves a higher cumulative reward ($6.602 \times 10^6$ versus
$6.549 \times 10^6$). This is consistent with the reward function design: the DQN
optimises for throughput reward rather than raw fairness, and in this regime it
concentrates visits on the highest-throughput sensors rather than distributing attention
equally. The difference is statistically significant at both compact grid sizes
($100 \times 100$: $t = 3.12$, $p = 0.029$ against SF-Aware; $t = 3.26$, $p = 0.020$
against Nearest; $300 \times 300$: $t = 2.95$, $p = 0.018$ against SF-Aware; $t = 3.43$,
$p = 0.009$ against Nearest).

\paragraph{Intermediate grid ($500 \times 500$, SF9/SF11 regime).}
At $500 \times 500$, the DQN's throughput advantage is most pronounced (+6.7\% over
SF-Aware Greedy, +5.7\% over Nearest Greedy in reward), and its fairness advantage over
the Nearest Greedy becomes visible ($\mathcal{J} = 0.9824$ versus $0.9753$). This regime
represents the sweet spot for the protocol-aware policy: the combination of SF diversity
and intermediate distances creates the Capture Effect opportunities that the DQN has
learned to exploit.

\paragraph{Large grid ($1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$, SF11/SF12 regime).}
At $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$, the SF-Aware Greedy achieves a higher raw cumulative reward
than the DQN ($4.992 \times 10^6$ versus $4.853 \times 10^6$), while the DQN leads on
Jain's Index ($0.9044$ versus $0.9001$ versus $0.8856$). The high inter-seed variance
(DQN reward std $\approx 605{,}000$) means neither reward nor fairness differences are
statistically significant at this scale ($p > 0.57$). The DQN's lower raw reward reflects
its fairness-penalty overhead: the global buffer-overflow penalty applied to all action
types causes the agent to sacrifice throughput from high-rate sensors to service
underserved sensors at the periphery, a behaviour that reduces cumulative reward but
improves the Jain's Index.

\paragraph{Fairness vs.\ throughput trade-off.}
The crossover at $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$---where SF-Aware Greedy leads on reward but
the DQN leads on fairness---illustrates a fundamental tension in multi-objective
optimisation. The DQN has been trained to value fairness through the AoI urgency penalty
and buffer-overflow cost; in a large, sparse network this correctly prioritises neglected
sensors but at the cost of total throughput. Whether this trade-off is desirable depends
on the application: time-sensitive monitoring applications (disaster response, public
safety) should prefer the DQN's fairness-first policy, whereas throughput-maximisation
applications (bulk data backhaul) might prefer the SF-Aware Greedy at $1{,}000 \times
1{,}000$ scale.
