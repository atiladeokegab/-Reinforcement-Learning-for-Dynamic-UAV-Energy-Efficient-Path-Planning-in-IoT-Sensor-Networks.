\label{sec:scalability}

introduced in Section~\ref{sec:link_budget}. Table~\ref{tab:grid_summary} reports

\paragraph{Trivial regime (100$\times$100).}
All three agents achieve equivalent performance ($\text{NDR} \approx 80\%$,
$\mathcal{J} > 0.96$). At this scale every sensor lies within SF7 collection range from
any UAV position; any routing strategy can service the full network within a single
episode, so protocol-aware decision-making provides no marginal value.

\paragraph{Feasible regime (300$\times$300).}
A sharp performance differentiation emerges. SF-Aware Greedy achieves $+16.2$\,pp higher
collection rate (40.1\% vs.\ 23.9\%) and $\mathcal{J} = 0.547$ vs.\ $0.327$ for the DQN
($d = -1.65$, $p = 0.052$). The DQN and Nearest-Sensor Greedy are near-identical
(23.9\% vs.\ 23.4\%), indicating the DQN has not learned to replicate SFGreedy's explicit
SF-directed routing at this scale. The 300$\times$300 regime is where sensors are
intermittently within SF7/SF9 range, and the SF-Aware Greedy's hard-coded criterion to
route toward the lowest-SF sensor exploits this most efficiently.

\paragraph{Marginal regime (500$\times$500).}
SF-Aware Greedy maintains the lead ($\mathcal{J} = 0.261$ vs.\ DQN $0.125$;
$d = -1.84$, $p = 0.039$, statistically significant). At this scale the majority of
sensors are in SF11/SF12 range, and visits yield fewer bytes per step. DQN and Nearest
Greedy converge to similar performance ($0.125$ vs.\ $0.133$), both substantially
below SFGreedy.

\paragraph{Infeasible regime (1,000$\times$1,000).}
Per-sensor collection rates collapse for all agents. The DQN achieves the lowest mean
coverage rate (1.2\%), with extremely high variance ($\sigma = 2.1$). The DQN's
mean Jain's index of $0.244$ is misleading: it arises from degenerate seeds in which the
agent hovers near the deployment origin and collects from only 1--2 nearby sensors
with equal allocation, driving $\mathcal{J}$ artificially to 1.0 while starving the
remaining 18 sensors. The NNGreedy is more consistent at this scale (NDR $= 5.6\%$,
$\sigma = 0.8$), routing greedily to the nearest sensor regardless of SF or battery state.
SF-Aware Greedy achieves the highest coverage (12.3\%) by consistently routing toward the
lowest-SF (closest) sensors, but still fails to visit more than a small fraction of the
network. All three agents confirm the physical infeasibility prediction: the 2,100-step
energy budget ($d_{\max} \approx 15{,}782$\,m) falls far short of the TSP tour at this
scale ($\approx 44{,}700$\,m).

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The grid-size sweep establishes the operational envelope empirically. A single-UAV system
is practically feasible at 300$\times$300 and below, and physically infeasible at
$1{,}000\times1{,}000$ regardless of policy quality. At the feasible scale (300$\times$300),
explicit SF-aware routing (SF-Aware Greedy) outperforms the DQN's learned policy. This
result motivates the future work on attention-based policies that can explicitly represent
sensor topology and SF assignments (Section~\ref{sec:future_work}): such an architecture
could in principle learn a routing strategy equivalent to or better than the hard-coded
greedy criterion, while also generalising to novel sensor distributions and multi-UAV
coordination.
