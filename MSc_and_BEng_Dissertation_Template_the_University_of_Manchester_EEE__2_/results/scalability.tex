\subsection{Scalability Analysis}

\subsubsection{Sensor-Count Scalability}

Figure~\ref{fig:scalability} shows the DQN advantage in cumulative reward and Jain's
fairness index as a function of sensor count ($N \in \{10, 20, 30, 40\}$, grid fixed at
$500 \times 500$). The DQN advantage grows with $N$: from $+2.3\%$ reward
at $N=10$ ($p < 0.001$, Cohen's $d = 2.52$, large effect) to $+9.0\%$ at $N=30$
($p = 0.021$, $d = 0.78$) and $+8.6\%$ at $N=40$ ($p = 0.043$, $d = 0.54$,
medium effect) over SF-Aware Greedy, with Jain's fairness advantage rising from
$+0.02\%$ to $+4.2\%$. All four density conditions reach statistical significance ($p < 0.05$).

Absolute metric values and the DQN advantage trend across sensor counts are shown in
Appendix~\ref{app:additional_figures} (Figure~\ref{fig:scalability}).

\subsubsection{Grid-Size Scalability}

A second sweep varied the operational area from $100\times100$ to $1{,}000\times1{,}000$
cells ($N=20$ sensors, ten seeds per condition), spanning three LoRa regimes:
SF7-dominant at $100\times100$, SF9--SF11 at $500\times500$, and SF11/SF12 at
$1{,}000\times1{,}000$ where ADR latency is most severe.

Table~\ref{tab:grid_summary} summarises results. At compact grids ($100\times100$,
$300\times300$), all agents achieve near-perfect fairness ($\mathcal{J} > 0.999$)
as sensors remain within SF7 range; the DQN achieves higher throughput
($+2.5\%$ at $100\times100$, $p < 0.001$; $+2.0\%$ at $300\times300$, $p < 0.001$)
by concentrating visits on the highest-rate sensors. At $500\times500$, the DQN's
protocol-aware repositioning yields the largest advantage ($+4.5\%$ reward,
$+0.75\%$ fairness over SF-Aware Greedy, $p = 0.156$).

fundamental fairness--throughput trade-off: the DQN's buffer-overflow penalty drives
it to service underserved peripheral sensors, sacrificing total throughput but
maintaining higher Jain's Index. Applications requiring equitable coverage (disaster
response, public safety) should prefer the DQN's fairness-first policy.
