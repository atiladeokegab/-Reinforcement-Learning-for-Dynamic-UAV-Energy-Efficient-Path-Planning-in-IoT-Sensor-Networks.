\subsection{Component Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

Four ablation conditions were run across five scales (100${\times}$100 to 500${\times}$500), 50 seeds each, to isolate each component's contribution. A1 removes the Capture Effect so all same-SF co-transmissions result in packet loss, disabling the collision-resolution path entirely. A2 switches to instant ADR ($\lambda{=}1.0$), updating each sensor's SF immediately to the value optimal for the current RSSI with no EMA smoothing; this is an upper bound on ADR performance, not a design target. A3 zeros per-sensor urgency and buffer-fill features at inference, so the policy receives no Age-of-Information signal. A4 substitutes a separately trained DQN that saw only $500{\times}500$, $N{=}20$ during training, with no grid-size or sensor-count variation.

Results are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}
(Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_combined}, Appendix~\ref{app:additional_figures}).
NDR is sensor coverage (\% of sensors visited), consistent with
the scalability analysis in Section~\ref{sec:multi_seed}.

\medskip

Protocol components (A1, A2) have negligible effect across all scales. Removing the
capture effect (A1) produces no statistically significant change at any condition
($p > 0.27$ throughout), which confirms that channel collisions are not a meaningful
bottleneck for the evaluated sensor densities. Instant ADR (A2) yields a marginal
improvement at $300{\times}300$ ($p{=}0.039$, $d{=}-0.42$, small), consistent with A2
being an upper bound on ADR performance; the effect disappears at larger scales
where coverage is already limited by navigation capacity rather than link quality.

Removing AoI features (A3) degrades NDR at every scale, and effect size grows with task difficulty. At $(100{\times}100, N{=}10)$ NDR falls from $100.0\%$ to $75.4\%$ ($p < 0.001$, $d = 0.95$, large): urgency signals matter even when routing is not the binding constraint, because without them the policy revisits already-collected sensors instead of seeking out new ones. At intermediate scales the degradation continues: $93.5\%$ vs $99.7\%$ at $200{\times}200$ ($d = 0.46$, small) and $42.0\%$ vs $51.7\%$ at $300{\times}300$ ($d = 0.50$, medium). At the hardest condition NDR collapses from $13.0\%$ to $4.4\%$ ($p < 0.001$, $d = 1.07$, large). The urgency proxy (buffer fill level combined with time since last collection) is what the policy uses to prioritise which sensor to visit; without it, routing degrades as sensors deplete and the policy cannot distinguish which ones still need attention. AoI features are load-bearing at every scale.

A4 outperforms the Full Model at the three largest conditions, and effect sizes grow with scale: $d = 1.35$ at $300{\times}300$, $1.38$ at $400{\times}400$, $1.69$ at $500{\times}500$ (all $p < 10^{-8}$). At $N{=}50$ the specialist reaches $26.4\%$ NDR vs the Full Model's $13.0\%$. This is in-distribution overfit, not evidence of a better policy. A4 was trained exclusively on $(500{\times}500, N{=}20)$ and is tested in conditions that closely match that setup. Domain randomisation forces the Full Model to spread capacity across 16 conditions spanning a $25\times$ range in grid area and a $5\times$ range in sensor count; per-condition performance is correspondingly lower. The scalability sweep (Section~\ref{sec:multi_seed}) shows the payoff: the Full DR model works at grid sizes and sensor counts the A4 model never saw during training.

At large scale the binding constraint is the representational capacity of the
flat-MLP policy, not the protocol design. Section~\ref{sec:relational} addresses this directly with the Relational-RL comparison. Per-condition extended figures are in
Appendix~\ref{app:additional_figures}.
