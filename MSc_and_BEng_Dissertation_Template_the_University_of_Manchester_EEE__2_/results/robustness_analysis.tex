\subsection{Robustness and Generalisation Analysis}

\subsubsection{Sim-to-Real Perturbation Tests}

To quantify the sensitivity of the trained policy to real-world deployment mismatches,
three domain shifts were applied at evaluation time (no retraining):

\noindent\textbf{GPS position noise}: Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma)$ was added
to the UAV position observation. At realistic accuracy levels ($\sigma=1$, i.e.\ 10\,m),
reward degradation is negligible ($+8.8\%$ change, within seed variance). Even at
$\sigma=10$ (100\,m error) performance is stable, confirming the policy is robust to
GPS uncertainty. Jain's Index remains 1.00 across all noise levels, indicating fairness
is not affected.

\noindent\textbf{Path-loss model mismatch}: Evaluating with path-loss exponent shifted
by up to $\Delta n = +1.0$ from the training value of $n=3.8$ produces reward degradation
of only $-0.7\%$, confirming the policy is robust to moderate channel model errors.

\noindent\textbf{Duty-cycle tightening}: Reducing sensor duty cycle from 10\% to 6\%
degrades reward by $-8.7\%$; tightening to 4\% degrades by $-14.2\%$. This is the most
significant sensitivity identified and reflects a genuine physical dependency: stricter
duty cycles reduce the number of transmission windows per step, fundamentally limiting
achievable throughput regardless of policy quality. The full per-condition breakdown is
provided in Appendix~\ref{app:sim_to_real}.

\subsubsection{Cross-Layout Generalisation}

The policy was trained exclusively on uniformly random sensor layouts via domain
randomisation. Table~\ref{tab:cross_layout} reports the DQN's reward advantage over
SF-Aware Greedy across six structurally distinct deployment patterns (all $N=20$,
$500\times500$, 5 seeds), assessing whether the learned policy transfers to
out-of-distribution layouts.

Table~\ref{tab:cross_layout} reports the per-layout results. The DQN generalises
successfully to grid-aligned ($+8.3\%$, $p=0.001$) and ring ($+5.4\%$, $p=0.001$)
layouts. However, the policy \emph{fails} on the two-cluster layout ($-11.3\%$, $p=0.001$):
the DQN's intermediate-positioning strategy---optimal for uniform random layouts---is
suboptimal when all sensors are co-located in two dense groups, where the SF-Aware
Greedy's visit-nearest heuristic is near-optimal because no repositioning overhead is needed.
The root cause is a training distribution mismatch: uniform domain randomisation never
generates bimodal density profiles. Adding clustered layouts to the curriculum would enable
a context-sensitive switching strategy.
Full per-layout bar charts are in Appendix~\ref{app:sim_to_real}.

\subsubsection{Hardware-in-the-Loop Preparation Analysis}
\label{sec:hil}

Two additional domain shifts inherent to physical UAV platforms were evaluated to complete
the pre-deployment robustness profile.

\noindent\textbf{Systematic wind drift.}
A constant drift vector (random direction, fixed per episode) of magnitude
$\delta_w \in \{0, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ grid-units per step was applied to the UAV's
position and observation.

\noindent\textbf{Observation latency.}
The observation seen by the policy was delayed by $k \in \{0, 1, 2, 4\}$ timesteps,
modelling avionics processing delays of $0$--$400$\,ms (typical DJI SDK round-trip:
$50$--$200$\,ms).

Results are in Appendix~\ref{app:additional_figures} (Figure~\ref{fig:hil}).
At $\delta_w = 1.0$ ($10$\,m/step) reward falls by $6.6\%$, identifying one grid cell
per step as the primary sensitivity threshold; at extreme drift ($\delta_w = 2.0$) the
impact is small ($-0.5\%$), consistent with the steep $1/d^4$ RSSI gradient beyond
sensor range. Observation latency at all tested levels ($k = 1$--$4$ steps) produces
negligible reward change, confirming immunity to delays up to $400$\,ms---a direct
benefit of the four-frame observation stack.

Together these results define a \emph{HIL readiness profile}: the policy is robust to
GPS noise, path-loss mismatch, duty-cycle variation, wind drift up to $10$\,m/step, and
observation latency up to $400$\,ms. The primary remaining uncertainty is the joint
effect of simultaneous perturbations.
