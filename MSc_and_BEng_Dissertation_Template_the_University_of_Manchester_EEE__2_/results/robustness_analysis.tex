\subsection{Robustness and Generalisation Analysis}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsubsection{Sim-to-Real Perturbation Tests}

To quantify the sensitivity of the trained policy to real-world deployment mismatches,
three domain shifts were applied at evaluation time (no retraining):

\noindent\textbf{GPS position noise}: Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma)$ was added
to the UAV position observation. At realistic accuracy levels ($\sigma=1$, i.e.\ 10\,m),
reward degradation is negligible ($+8.8\%$ change, within seed variance). Even at
$\sigma=10$ (100\,m error) performance is stable, confirming the policy is robust to
GPS uncertainty. Jain's Index remains 1.00 across all noise levels, indicating fairness
is not affected.

\noindent\textbf{Path-loss model mismatch}: Evaluating with path-loss exponent shifted
by up to $\Delta n = +1.0$ from the training value of $n=3.8$ produces reward degradation
of only $-0.7\%$, confirming the policy is robust to moderate channel model errors.

\noindent\textbf{Duty-cycle tightening}: Reducing sensor duty cycle from 10\% to 6\%
degrades reward by $-8.7\%$; tightening to 4\% degrades by $-14.2\%$. This is the most
significant sensitivity identified and reflects a genuine physical dependency: stricter
duty cycles reduce the number of transmission windows per step, limiting
achievable throughput regardless of policy quality. The full per-condition breakdown is
provided in Appendix~\ref{app:sim_to_real}.

\subsubsection{Cross-Layout Generalisation}

The policy was trained exclusively on uniformly random sensor layouts via domain
randomisation. Table~\ref{tab:cross_layout} reports the DQN's reward advantage over
SF-Aware Greedy across six structurally distinct deployment patterns (all $N=20$,
$500\times500$, $n=10$ seeds per layout, Welch's $t$-test with Cohen's $d$).

Table~\ref{tab:cross_layout} reveals three generalisation regimes.
\emph{Structured regular} (grid-aligned $+2.6\%$, $p=0.007$, $d=1.36$; ring $+3.0\%$,
$p=0.002$, $d=1.70$): large effects confirm reliable advantage on geometrically regular layouts.
\emph{Dispersed similar to training} (uniform random, 4-corner, single quadrant): no significant
difference ($p>0.50$ for all three), consistent with the training distribution covering
analogous spatial arrangements.
\emph{Bimodal} (two dense clusters, $-14.0\%$, $p<0.001$, $d=5.33$): the very large effect
identifies the precise failure boundary: bimodal distributions absent from the training
curriculum where the Greedy's visit-nearest heuristic is near-optimal.
This motivated the clustered layout curriculum extension described in
Section~\ref{sec:clustered_curriculum}.
Full per-layout bar charts are in Appendix~\ref{app:sim_to_real}.

\subsubsection{Clustered Layout Curriculum: A Negative Result}
\label{sec:clustered_curriculum}

To test whether the bimodal failure is addressable without architectural changes, the
domain-randomisation curriculum was extended to include structurally diverse sensor placements.
During fine-tuning from the 2\,M-step checkpoint, each episode independently samples one of
three layout distributions: 70\% uniform random (the original training distribution), 15\%
bimodal two-cluster, and 15\% four-corner clustered, using a reduced learning rate
$\eta = 10^{-5}$ for 500\,k additional timesteps.

Table~\ref{tab:cross_layout_ft} reports the before/after results for the two-cluster layout.

The curriculum fine-tuning does not close the bimodal gap: the two-cluster disadvantage
persists at $-15.1\%$ ($d=4.87$), unchanged from the original $-14.0\%$
($d=5.33$). Performance on the uniform-random layout improves marginally ($+3.6\%$ vs.\
$+2.7\%$ before fine-tuning), confirming that no catastrophic forgetting of the original
distribution occurred.

This negative result is itself informative: it demonstrates that the two-cluster failure is
\emph{not} a training distribution gap correctable by curriculum augmentation alone.
The root cause is architectural. The bimodal layout requires the agent to partition and
sequence coverage across two spatially separated clusters, a planning task analogous to the
Travelling Salesman Problem with two depots. The fixed-capacity MLP with frame stacking has
no explicit mechanism to encode cluster identity or inter-cluster routing; the
Greedy's visit-nearest heuristic trivially produces near-optimal behaviour in this regime.
Resolving this boundary condition requires either an attention-based architecture capable
of encoding sensor topology~\cite{kool2018attention}, or a graph-neural-network
policy~\cite{cappart2023combinatorial} that explicitly represents the deployment graph.
This is identified as a primary architectural extension in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

\subsubsection{Hardware-in-the-Loop Preparation Analysis}
\label{sec:hil}

Two further domain shifts were evaluated (Figure~\ref{fig:hil}, Appendix~\ref{app:additional_figures}).
Systematic wind drift ($\delta_w \in \{0, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ grid-units/step) reduces reward by
$6.6\%$ at $\delta_w = 1.0$ (10\,m/step), identifying one grid cell per step as the primary
sensitivity threshold; beyond this the steep $1/d^4$ RSSI gradient limits further degradation
($-0.5\%$ at $\delta_w = 2.0$). Observation latency ($k \in \{1, 2, 4\}$ steps, modelling
$50$--$400$\,ms DJI SDK round-trips) produces negligible reward change, confirming immunity to
avionics delays, a direct benefit of the four-frame observation stack. Together with the GPS,
path-loss, and duty-cycle results, these define the HIL readiness profile.
