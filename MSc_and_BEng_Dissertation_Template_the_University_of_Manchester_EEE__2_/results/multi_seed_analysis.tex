\subsection{Multi-Seed Comparative Analysis}

\subsubsection{Network Coverage}

Across all four sensor-count conditions and all five seeds, both the DQN agent and the
two greedy baselines achieve complete network coverage (100\%) in every episode, with one
exception: the DQN agent achieves 97.5\% coverage on the most challenging condition
($N = 40$, seed 1337). This confirms that the terminal unvisited-sensor penalty is
effective at driving the agent to explore the full sensor field rather than camping on
a profitable cluster, and that all three algorithms are equally capable of full coverage
at the densities tested.

\subsubsection{Data Throughput and Cumulative Reward}

Table~\ref{tab:reward_summary} reports the mean cumulative reward and mean energy
efficiency across five seeds for each agent at each sensor count. The DQN agent
outperforms both baselines on cumulative reward at every network density, with the
margin growing as the network becomes denser. At $N = 10$, the advantage over SF-Aware
Greedy is modest (+2.7\%), consistent with the near-trivial nature of the routing
problem at low sensor counts. As density increases to $N = 40$, the DQN advantage
grows to +7.4\% over SF-Aware Greedy and +6.1\% over Nearest Sensor Greedy, indicating
that the learned policy exploits spreading-factor diversity more effectively as the
number of concurrent transmission opportunities increases.

\subsubsection{Fairness}

Jain's fairness index $\mathcal{J}$ is computed over the per-sensor data collection
rates at episode end, where $\mathcal{J} = 1$ indicates perfectly equal collection
across all sensors. Table~\ref{tab:fairness_summary} reports the mean Jain's index
for each agent and sensor count.

\noindent Three observations merit discussion. First, at $N = 10$ all agents achieve
near-perfect fairness ($\mathcal{J} \approx 0.9997$), as the sparse network gives the
UAV sufficient time to fully service every sensor within the battery budget.

Second, fairness degrades monotonically with sensor count for all agents. This is a
consequence of the fixed episode length: as $N$ grows, the UAV must prioritise higher-
throughput sensors (those with favourable SF assignments) over distant, low-rate sensors
that are geometrically harder to reach within the battery budget. This is not a failure
of the reward function but an intrinsic constraint of the single-UAV, fixed-horizon
formulation.

Third, the DQN agent achieves a higher mean Jain's index than SF-Aware Greedy at every
density ($+0.03\%$ at $N=10$, $+0.41\%$ at $N=20$, $+1.7\%$ at $N=30$, $+3.9\%$ at
$N=40$). While these margins are modest at low density, the growing advantage at high
density is significant: at $N = 40$, the DQN achieves $\mathcal{J} = 0.6717$ versus
$\mathcal{J} = 0.6462$ for SF-Aware Greedy. This suggests that the DQN's learned
exploration policy---driven by the new-sensor discovery bonus and starvation
penalty---distributes attention more evenly across the sensor field than the purely
myopic greedy heuristic.

\subsubsection{Energy Efficiency}

Across all agents and all conditions, the total energy consumed per episode is
effectively constant at $\approx 268.5$\,Wh, confirming that every agent fully depletes
the UAV battery regardless of policy. The differentiating factor is therefore the
\emph{amount of data collected per unit energy}. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:reward_summary},
the DQN agent's energy efficiency advantage over SF-Aware Greedy grows from
+2.5\,B/Wh at $N = 10$ to +15.3\,B/Wh at $N = 40$ (+7.3\%). This gain arises because
the DQN learns to position the UAV at locations that allow concurrent multi-sensor
collection across orthogonal spreading factors, an opportunity that the greedy heuristics
exploit only partially.
